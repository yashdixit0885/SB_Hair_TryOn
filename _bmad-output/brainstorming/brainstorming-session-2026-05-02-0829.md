## Phase 1 — What If Scenarios (in progress)

### Round 1 — Features: "Preview is dynamic, not static"

**[Features #1]: 90-Day Fade Simulator** ⭐ *V1 confirmed (user-flagged)*
_Concept_: Slider that shows the chosen color from "freshly done" through 90 days of fade, with washes-per-week as a variable. User sees what maintenance commitment they're signing up for, not just the day-1 result.
_Novelty_: Existing try-on tools all show the salon-day result. None acknowledge that hair color is a depreciating asset — turning the #1 post-purchase regret ("it faded so fast") into a pre-purchase informed choice.

**[Features #2]: Multi-Lighting Toggle** ⭐ *V1 confirmed (user-flagged)*
_Concept_: Render the same color under salon lights / daylight / fluorescent office / warm restaurant / morning bathroom mirror. One-tap switch between lighting environments.
_Novelty_: Directly kills the "this looked different in the salon" complaint that drives the most refunds and bad reviews in the entire color category. Nobody else does this because it's hard — it requires lighting-aware rendering, not just texture mapping.

**[Features #3]: Root Growth Preview**
_Concept_: "Here's you in 6 weeks if you don't touch up." Visualize regrowth band based on user's natural color (which we'd ask) and chosen new color.
_Novelty_: Converts a passive product purchase into a calendar-aware reorder loop — directly drives Sally Beauty's repeat revenue.

**[Features #4]: Seasonal Palette Reader**
_Concept_: Show how a chosen color reads against summer tan vs. winter pale skin. Could pair with location/time-of-year auto-detection.
_Novelty_: Reframes "what color suits me" from a single answer to a season-specific recommendation, opening the door to seasonal recoloring as a habit.

### Round 2 — UX Moments: "Reframe as recurring micro-entertainment, not just purchase tool"

**[UX #5]: Salon Waiting-Room Mode** ⭐ *V1 (user-flagged)*
_Concept_: Geofenced trigger — when phone detects user inside a Sally Beauty store, app surfaces a "try before you grab the box" mode tied to the SKUs physically on the shelf in front of them. Camera optionally scans a box and renders that exact shade.
_Novelty_: Collapses the "look at boxes → guess → buy → regret" loop into "look at box → see it on me → buy with confidence." Uses Sally's physical retail footprint as the distribution wedge competitors (TikTok filters, online-only brands) can't replicate.

**[UX #6]: Pre-Event Try-On** ⭐ *V2 (user-flagged)*
_Concept_: Calendar/event-aware mode — "You've got a wedding in 3 weeks. Here are 5 looks that go with the dress in your last Pinterest pin / the outfit you saved." Plans the color around an actual life moment.
_Novelty_: Anchors the color decision to a *deadline and an outfit*, which reduces decision paralysis. Creates a natural retention loop tied to life calendar, not app sessions.

**[UX #7]: Bestie Consult / Social Approval** ⭐ *V3 (user-flagged)*
_Concept_: Share-to-friend with a vote mechanic — "which of these 3 should I do?" Friends vote in-app or via shared link, app shows live tally and can recommend the consensus pick.
_Novelty_: Turns a solo decision into a social ritual. Built-in viral loop (every share recruits a non-user voter). Saves the "social risk" anxiety that kills 60%+ of color experiments.

**Sequencing rationale (user-flagged):** V1 Salon → V2 Pre-event → V3 Social. Each version expands trust, lock-in, and ambition: in-store wedge first (instant reward, low risk), then deepen with personal context, then go viral via social.

### Round 3 — Business Model: "Two-sided marketplace, users always free"

**🔒 Confirmed business model (user-stated, treat as constraint going forward):**
- Sally Beauty partners with (does not own) a network of salons
- App is 100% free to consumers — no consumer revenue ever
- Revenue from two sides: (a) **salons** pay to be discoverable + drive bookings; (b) **product brands** (Wella, Schwarzkopf, L'Oréal Pro, Clairol, etc.) pay to feature their colors in the try-on engine
- App is a **precursor funnel** — every successful try-on should ideally route the user into a salon visit for the real work

**[BizModel #8]: Two-Sided Marketplace** ⭐ *Core model (user-confirmed)*
_Concept_: Consumers free; salons + product brands pay. App connects "I want this look" → "Here's the product to use + the salon to go to."
_Novelty_: Most try-on apps are either DTC funnels (one brand) or pure novelty (TikTok). This is the only one with simultaneous brand-side AND service-side monetization, which means it's defensible as a *channel* not a product.

**[BizModel #9]: ~~Cross-Brand Color Discrimination Engine~~ → REVISED to Color-First Browse + Review-Based Differentiation** ⭐ *Core model (user-revised)*
_Concept_: User browses by **color** (not by brand). Same color = same render across brands ("copper blonde looks like copper blonde, period"). When user picks a color, app shows: (a) their look, (b) every brand that offers this color, (c) per-brand user ratings — fade speed, durability, post-application feel, real-world results. Some rare proprietary colors only show 1–2 brands → "this is a unique look only [Brand X] does."
_Novelty_: Technical simplicity (one render per color, not per SKU) + the differentiation moat moves to **user-generated review data** which compounds over time. Brands compete on actual customer outcomes, not rendering claims. Becomes the "Yelp + Pinterest for hair color" — the more users post outcomes, the harder the database is to copy.

**[BizModel #9b]: Proprietary-Color Spotlight**
_Concept_: When a color is only offered by 1–2 brands (a true proprietary blend), the app surfaces it as "Only [Brand] does this — truly unique look." Becomes a discovery surface for niche/specialty brands and a status signal for users who want to look one-of-a-kind.
_Novelty_: Turns brand exclusivity into a *consumer-facing feature* instead of a hidden moat. Specialty brands (like indie or salon-only lines) get a way to stand out without massive marketing spend, and Sally captures their advertising dollars in exchange.

**[BizModel #10]: Salon Discovery + Profile Layer** ⭐ *V1 requirement (user-flagged)*
_Concept_: Each "go to a salon" recommendation surfaces full salon profile: services offered, price range, certified-for-which-brands, recent client photo gallery, reviews from other app users (specifically rating the *color result*, not the haircut or coffee).
_Novelty_: Existing salon discovery (Google, Yelp, Booksy) reviews salons in general. A color-specific review filter ("how good is this salon at copper?") is unique and directly serves the conversion moment. Becomes a *Yelp for color outcomes*.

**[BizModel #11]: Color → SKU → Salon Attribution Loop**
_Concept_: When a user tries a color in-app, then books a salon visit through the app, then the salon uses a specific brand's product on them — Sally tracks this end-to-end. Salons get verified leads they pay for; brands see real conversion data; users get loyalty rewards.
_Novelty_: Closes the attribution loop nobody else has. Brands currently have NO IDEA which marketing dollar drove which in-chair purchase. Sally would own the data layer.

**[BizModel #12]: Try-Booth In-Store Stations**
_Concept_: Physical "try a color" tablet stations inside partner salons themselves (not just Sally retail). Walk-in, snap, see, *then* sit in the chair. Salon converts more upsells; Sally gets in-front-of-customer placement at the highest-intent moment.
_Novelty_: Inverts the funnel — instead of app→salon, this is salon→app→better service. Salon owners would *pay* for it because it raises ticket size.

### Round 4 — Technical Approach: "Simplest thing that works" (user-corrected)

**🔒 Confirmed technical principle (user-stated, treat as constraint going forward):**
- Same color name = same render across brands. Do NOT attempt per-brand rendering fidelity.
- Brand differentiation lives in the **review data layer** (fade speed, durability, real-world results), not in the pixel-level rendering.
- Proprietary/unique colors are flagged as such (only 1–2 brands offer them).
- UX must be extremely simple — "browse colors → tap a color → see your look + brand options + ratings." No more.

**[Tech #13]: Color-First Browsing**
_Concept_: Default UX is a curated grid of color options (not a brand list). User taps a color → instantly sees themselves with it → list of brands offering it scrolls below with star ratings + key stats (avg fade weeks, "would recommend" %).
_Novelty_: Every existing try-on app forces the user to pick a brand first (because the apps are funded by single brands). Color-first browsing is only possible because Sally is brand-neutral — and it matches how users actually think ("I want copper" not "I want Wella").

**[Tech #14]: Review Schema Tied to Outcomes (not feelings)**
_Concept_: User reviews are *structured*, not freeform — sliders for "how long it lasted (weeks)," "how close to the preview (1–5)," "how much it damaged my hair (1–5)," plus an after-photo upload (optional but incentivized with rewards points). Freeform comment is a small box, not the focus.
_Novelty_: Existing beauty reviews are mostly "love it!" / "hated it" — useless for decisions. Outcome-anchored reviews produce a *queryable dataset* that drives recommendations, brand rankings, and trust over time. This dataset is the long-term moat.

**[Tech #15]: Render Engine Choice = Boring & Off-the-Shelf**
_Concept_: Pick the cheapest, fastest, well-supported third-party hair-color try-on SDK (Perfect Corp YouCam, ModiFace, Banuba, Snap AR Lens) for V1 rendering. Don't build it. Don't customize per-brand. Don't worry about spectral fidelity.
_Novelty_: Reframes the engineering bet — the app's defensibility isn't the renderer (which we treat as commodity), it's the **two-sided marketplace + review database** sitting on top. Engineering effort goes into the marketplace, not the camera.

**[Tech #16]: Hair Texture Inclusivity from Day 1**
_Concept_: Pick the SDK that has the best published track record on textured hair (type 4, coily, locs, braids, recently treated). Make this an explicit vendor-evaluation criterion, not an afterthought.
_Novelty_: Sally Beauty's actual customer demographic skews more textured-hair than mainstream beauty apps. A renderer that fails on type 4 hair will tank trust on day 1. Treating this as a hard requirement up front avoids the "let's add diversity in V2" trap that sinks beauty apps repeatedly.

### Round 5 — Risks & Cold-Start (user-resolved)

**🔒 Confirmed MVP UX constraint (user-stated):**
- **Upload-only flow for V1.** No live camera mode. Reason: prevents users from expecting in-camera live AR color experimentation, and keeps the user firmly in the "deliberate try-on" mental model rather than a TikTok-filter mental model. Live camera is a V2+ consideration.

**[UX #17]: Photo Upload-Only Flow (V1)** ⭐ *V1 constraint (user-flagged)*
_Concept_: User uploads a still photo. App processes server-side or on-device, returns the colored result. No live camera. No real-time AR.
_Novelty_: Almost every competitor opens with "tap to use camera live." Sally deliberately doesn't — which actually positions the product as more *considered* and *salon-grade* vs. the toy/filter category. Sets the right expectation: "this is for planning a real color appointment, not a 30-second filter."

**[Risk #18]: Cold-Start Review Bootstrap (3-phase plan)** ⭐ *V1 strategy (user-flagged)*
_Concept_: Three layered review sources from day 1, transitioning over time:
  - **Salon reviews:** ingest directly from Google Reviews (and possibly Yelp/Facebook) for each partner salon. Display with attribution ("Source: Google Reviews").
  - **Brand color reviews:** scrape from each brand's product page on their own website. Display with attribution ("Source: [Brand] product reviews").
  - **Native review collection:** every booking made via the app triggers (a) automated review-request email a few days post-appointment, (b) in-app push for review submission. Tracks the booking date as the trigger.
  - **Transition:** once a salon or color has a critical mass of native reviews, switch primary display to "SB Users said..." with the external sources demoted to secondary tab.
_Novelty_: Solves chicken-and-egg without faking data, without paying for fake reviews, and without launching to silence. Plus, the *act* of pulling external reviews creates an instant data integration story Sally can talk about ("we already index 50,000 salon reviews on launch day").

**[Risk #19]: Review Source Attribution / Trust Layer**
_Concept_: Every review (no matter the source) is labeled clearly: "Google Review," "[Brand] verified buyer," "SB User who booked through this app." Users see exactly where the trust is coming from.
_Novelty_: Most aggregators hide their sources to seem authoritative. Transparent attribution actually *increases* trust, especially in a beauty category that's been burned by fake reviews. Could become a marketing point: "Sally Beauty doesn't fake reviews — we show you exactly who's saying what."

**[Risk #20]: Booking-Triggered Review Loop (Engagement Hook)**
_Concept_: The booking → review-request loop is also the app's primary retention mechanism. Even if the user only opens the app once a year for a color, the post-appointment review request keeps a thread of engagement and brings them back to see "did anyone else try this color since I did?"
_Novelty_: Most beauty apps die because there's no reason to come back between purchases. Tying re-engagement to a *contribution* moment (your review helps the next user) is more sustainable than push notifications about new shades.

### Phase 1 Wrap — Final V1 Scope (user-confirmed)

**V1 includes:** photo upload flow, color-first browse, color render via off-the-shelf SDK, brand options + reviews per color, salon discovery + profile + booking, **90-day fade simulator**, **multi-lighting toggle**, salon waiting-room mode (geofenced), cold-start review bootstrap (Google + brand-site scrape + post-booking review collection).

**V2:** pre-event try-on (calendar integration).
**V3:** bestie consult social vote.

**Bias-to-ship rationale (user-flagged):** Faster build → faster deploy → faster real user feedback → better next iteration. Avoid analysis paralysis. Phase 1 ideation closes here so we can cluster and move toward the build phase.

The classic constraint prompt was applied (1 PM + 4 eng + 1 designer + 1 BD + 1 editorial; 16 weeks; 1 geography; 10 salons; 5 brands; no marketing spend). Output below is the V1 cut list, sequence, success metrics, and handoff packet for the next skill.

### Riskiest Assumption (test before building anything else)

> Users will trust the color preview enough to walk into a salon and book a real appointment based on what they saw.

**Cheap test:** Figma + 1 working renderer demo, 20 in-store interviews at 1 Sally location. Look for ≥40% hard-yes. <20% means the product needs a different shape.

### V1 Scope (13 features ship in 16 weeks)

Photo upload flow · Color-first browse (~30 curated) · Off-the-shelf SDK render · Per-color brand list + ratings · 90-day fade simulator · Multi-lighting toggle (3 presets) · Salon discovery + profile · Salon booking deep-link handoff (NOT in-app) · Reviews from Google Places + 5 partner brand feeds · Post-booking review request · Source-attribution labels · Web + responsive mobile (single codebase) · Privacy-compliant photo handling.

### Deferred (V2+)

Live camera mode · Pre-event try-on · Bestie social vote · In-app booking · Geofence triggers · Brand-site review scraping (legal gray) · Stylist seed reviews · Attribution analytics dashboards · In-store kiosks · Seasonal/root-growth features · Native iOS/Android apps.

### Build Sequence (16 weeks, parallel tracks)

- **Wks 0–2:** BD signs LOIs (5 brands + 10 salons); editorial drafts taxonomy; legal drafts policies; eng evaluates 3 SDKs.
- **Wks 3–6:** Core build (upload, render, color browse, brand list).
- **Wks 7–10:** Marketplace + reviews (salon profiles, brand feeds, attribution).
- **Wks 11–13:** Differentiating features (fade, lighting, post-booking triggers).
- **Wks 14–15:** Hardening + 50-user closed beta + policy publish.
- **Wk 16:** Public launch (1 geography, 10 salons, 5 brands, 30 colors).

## Session Summary and Insights

### Key Achievements

- **20 ideas** generated across 6 dimensions in a single session
- **MVP shape locked** — 13 features in, 11 features out, with rationale for each cut
- **8 de-risk action items** identified with owners assigned
- **6 V1 success metrics** defined so the team knows what "done" means
- **Differentiator pitch** crystalized in one line
- **Riskiest assumption** named, with a cheap test to validate it

### Creative Breakthroughs

1. **The "moat is the data, not the renderer"** insight — switching the engineering bet from custom rendering to off-the-shelf SDK + outcome-anchored reviews collapsed technical risk while *increasing* defensibility.
2. **The "Yelp-for-color-outcomes"** reframe (Branch C) — turning brand-paid placement into a credibility problem solved by transparent reviews instead of fighting it.
3. **The cold-start bootstrap** (Google + brand feeds → native) — solving day-1 silence without faking data.
4. **The V1→V2→V3 sequencing** (salon → pre-event → social) — each version expands trust, lock-in, and ambition.

### Session Reflections

- **What worked:** Bias-to-ship discipline. User repeatedly course-corrected away from over-engineering ("we're making this more complicated than it needs to be"). This kept the V1 scope tight and shippable.
- **What worked:** Domain pivots every ~5 ideas (Features → UX → Business → Tech → Risk) prevented clustering on one dimension.
- **Surprise:** Most "risks" turned out to be organizational (BD, editorial, legal), not technical. The build itself is the easy part.
- **Watch-out for next phase:** The GTM / acquisition question is unresolved and was deliberately deferred to the Product Brief discussion.

### Creative Facilitation Narrative

This was a tightly-scoped, decisive session. User came in with a fuzzy idea ("upload a selfie, see hair colors") and left with a defensible V1 spec, an 8-item de-risk list, and a 16-week build sequence. The user's bias toward simplicity and shipping speed shaped the entire session — every time the facilitator suggested complexity (per-SKU spectral rendering, in-app booking, live camera), the user pushed back toward the simplest workable version. That instinct produced a stronger product than maximalist ideation would have.
